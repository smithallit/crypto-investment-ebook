# Part I — Foundations

## Chapter 1: What Is Cryptocurrency?

Cryptocurrency is digitally native value coordinated by distributed networks and secured by cryptography. Traditional finance usually relies on centralized institutions to maintain ledgers and enforce settlement. Crypto introduces alternative trust assumptions where users can independently verify transactions, ownership history, and protocol rules on open networks.

This does not remove risk—it changes where risk lives:
- Protocol and smart contract risk
- Key management and operational risk
- Market structure and liquidity risk
- Behavioral and decision-quality risk

### 1.1 Why Crypto Exists

Traditional systems can include:
- Settlement delays in some corridors
- Cross-border frictions
- Limited access in certain regions
- Closed financial infrastructure requiring institutional permission

Crypto emerged to enable:
- Open-access financial rails
- 24/7 market operation
- Programmable value through smart contracts
- Composable protocols that integrate into broader ecosystems

### 1.2 Core Properties

- **Digital scarcity:** Issuance rules are typically transparent and protocol-defined.
- **Verifiability:** Ledger history can often be audited independently on-chain.
- **Censorship resistance:** Transaction inclusion is not controlled by a single institution.
- **Composability:** Applications can integrate with each other on shared rails.
- **Global reach:** Participation is internet-native, while still subject to local law.

### 1.3 Categories of Crypto Assets

Use category-based analysis:
1. **Monetary/store-of-value assets**
2. **Smart contract platform assets**
3. **Utility/governance tokens**
4. **Stablecoins**
5. **Speculative/narrative-driven tokens**

Each category has different demand drivers, dilution dynamics, and risk profiles.

### 1.4 Investment Reality

Crypto combines venture-like uncertainty with public-market liquidity and continuous pricing. This creates rapid opportunity and rapid downside.

Your edge is process:
- Risk-first portfolio construction
- Disciplined position sizing
- Repeatable research workflow
- Consistent review and adaptation

4. Maximum crypto allocation allowed in your portfolio

## Chapter 2: Blockchain Fundamentals

A blockchain is a shared state machine maintained by distributed participants under consensus rules.

### 2.1 Transactions, Blocks, and State

- **Transaction:** Signed instruction from a user account.
- **Block:** Ordered transaction set accepted by network consensus.
- **State:** Current ledger condition after valid blocks are applied.

Ordering matters because it affects execution outcomes, slippage, liquidations, and MEV dynamics.

### 2.2 Consensus Mechanisms

**Proof of Work (PoW)**
- Security through computational expenditure
- Long operational history in major networks
- Energy and hardware distribution considerations

**Proof of Stake (PoS)**
- Security through staked capital and validator behavior
- Slashing and incentive design are central
- Concentration and governance quality are key evaluation factors

### 2.3 The Trilemma in Practice

Protocols balance:
- Decentralization
- Security
- Scalability

Tradeoffs are unavoidable. Evaluate them against the chain’s intended use case, not marketing claims.

### 2.4 Smart Contracts and Risk

Smart contracts are deterministic, but deterministic bugs are still bugs.

Primary risk vectors:
- Code defects
- Permission and upgrade key design
- Oracle dependencies
- Bridge dependencies
- Governance failures

Quality indicators include multiple audits, active bug bounties, and transparent incident response.

### 2.5 Fees, Throughput, and Scaling

Blockspace is scarce and fees are the pricing mechanism for that scarcity.

Compare ecosystems by:
- Typical and stress-period fees
- Time-to-finality
- Reliability under congestion
- Bridge and settlement assumptions
- Historical incident performance

### 2.6 Chapter Exercise

## Chapter 3: Market Structure and Crypto Cycles

Crypto markets are 24/7, fragmented, and highly reflexive. Liquidity and sentiment can reprice risk quickly.

### 3.1 Market Participants

Major participant groups:
- Long-term holders
- Discretionary active traders
- Market makers and quant firms
- Venture/insider allocation holders
- Retail momentum participants
- Protocol treasuries/foundations

Understanding who owns supply and why they might sell is essential.

### 3.2 Liquidity Regimes

Liquidity changes with:
- Macro conditions
- Volatility spikes
- Exchange confidence
- Narrative concentration

Quoted depth is not always executable depth under stress.

### 3.3 Typical Cycle Dynamics (Not Guaranteed)

1. Accumulation
2. Expansion
3. Euphoria
4. Distribution
5. Contraction/reset

Strategies should be regime-aware, with tighter risk in euphoric phases and quality bias in contractions.

### 3.4 Correlation and Contagion

During stress:
- Correlations often rise
- Stablecoin confidence may become systemic
- Forced deleveraging can propagate quickly

Portfolio design should assume contagion risk is possible.

### 3.5 Narrative vs Fundamentals

Narratives often drive short-term price velocity. Fundamentals influence durability over longer horizons.

Use narratives for context and timing sensitivity; use fundamentals for sizing and holding decisions.

Build a 4-regime playbook:
- Regime label
- Risk budget
- Stable reserve target
- Entry aggressiveness
- Rebalance cadence
- One non-negotiable rule

## Chapter 4: Wallets, Exchanges, and Security Fundamentals

Security is not a side topic in crypto—it is core portfolio infrastructure.

### 4.1 Custody Models

- **Custodial:** Convenience with higher counterparty risk.
- **Self-custody:** Control with higher operational responsibility.
- **Hybrid:** Common for balancing usability and resilience.

### 4.2 Wallet Segmentation

Use dedicated wallets for:
- Long-term holdings
- Routine operations
- DeFi interaction
- Experimental/high-risk activity

Segmentation reduces blast radius if one wallet is compromised.

### 4.3 Seed Phrase and Key Management

Core principles:
- Generate and store offline
- Never upload to cloud notes/email
- Never share with anyone
- Keep separated secure backups
- Test recovery before meaningful funding

### 4.4 Exchange Security Operations

- Strong unique passwords
- Authenticator/hardware-key 2FA
- Withdrawal allowlists
- Device/session monitoring
- Minimal API permissions
- Keep only operational balances on exchange

### 4.5 Scam and Social Engineering Defense

Common warning signs:
- Urgent pressure
- Impersonation/fake support
- Suspicious links and blind signing prompts
- Guaranteed-return promises

Rule: if rushed, pause and verify independently.

### 4.6 Chapter Exercise

Write your personal security blueprint:
- Custody split by percentage
- Wallet segmentation map
- Backup and recovery process
- Compromise response checklist
- Monthly security audit routine

# Part II — Investor Operating System

## Chapter 5: Risk Management First

Risk management is the primary driver of longevity. Surviving volatility is a prerequisite for compounding.

### 5.1 Risk Philosophy and Non-Negotiables

Define hard rules:
- Preserve capital first, grow second
- Never oversize a position
- Never increase risk to chase losses
- Never enter without invalidation criteria

Translate into limits:
- Max total crypto allocation
- Max position size
- Max speculative sleeve size
- Max drawdown threshold before de-risking

### 5.2 Risk Buckets and Portfolio Layers

Use four buckets:
- **Core**
- **Growth**
- **Speculative**
- **Stable reserve**

Each bucket needs target range, cap, rebalancing rules, and exit standards.

### 5.3 Drawdown Response Protocol

Pre-commit actions:
- **-10%:** review concentration/correlation
- **-20%:** trim weakest conviction exposures
- **-30%:** pause new speculative additions
- **-40%+:** full strategy/risk reset

### 5.4 Risk Dashboard

Track weekly:
- Volatility
- Drawdown
- Top-position concentration
- Stable reserve ratio
- Rule violations
- Portfolio heat

### 5.5 Behavioral Risk Controls

Common behavioral errors:
- FOMO entries
- Revenge trading
- Invalidation denial

Controls:
- Checklist-gated entries
- Cooldown periods
- Rule-violation log
- Monthly process audits

### 5.6 Chapter Exercise

Draft a one-page Risk Policy Statement:
- Limits

## Chapter 6: Position Sizing and Exposure Control

Position sizing converts analysis into controlled exposure.

### 6.1 Conviction-Tier Sizing

Classify each position:
- **Tier 1:** strongest conviction (largest permitted size)
- **Tier 2:** moderate conviction
- **Tier 3:** exploratory (smallest size)

Higher uncertainty/volatility/liquidity risk = smaller size.

### 6.2 Tranche-Based Entries

Avoid full-size entries in one trade:
- Plan 3–5 tranches
- Add only if thesis remains intact
- Stop adding when invalidation risk rises

### 6.3 Multi-Layer Exposure Caps

Control risk by:
- Asset cap
- Theme cap
- Chain/infrastructure cap
- Venue/custody cap
- Illiquidity cap

### 6.4 Correlation-Adjusted Sizing

If assets co-move in stress, treat them as a cluster with a cluster-level cap.

### 6.5 Dynamic Sizing Rules

Increase size only when:
- Thesis quality improves
- Liquidity supports scaling
- Portfolio heat remains within budget

Decrease size when:
- Thesis weakens
- Unlock/issuance pressure grows
- Correlation concentration increases

## Glossary

- **Allocation Band:** Allowed range around target weight.  
- **Contagion:** Risk transmission across assets/sectors during stress.  
- **Correlation Cluster:** Holdings likely to co-move, especially in risk-off periods.  
- **Drawdown:** Peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value.  
- **FDV:** Fully diluted valuation (price × max supply).  
- **Implementation Shortfall:** Difference between intended and realized execution.  
- **Invalidation:** Condition proving thesis is wrong.  
- **Liquidity Depth:** Executable size near current market prices.  
- **Portfolio Heat:** Aggregate current risk exposure.  
- **Risk Budget:** Maximum risk allocated to a strategy sleeve.  
- **Slippage:** Difference between expected and executed price.  
- **Thesis Drift:** Unacknowledged change from original investment logic.  
- **Time-to-Finality:** Time until settlement is highly irreversible.  
- **Volatility Regime:** Prevailing behavior of market fluctuation intensity.
