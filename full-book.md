## Chapter 1: What Is Cryptocurrency?

Cryptocurrency is a digital asset designed to transfer value across a network without relying on a central authority such as a bank. It combines cryptography, distributed systems, and economic incentives.

### 1.1 Why Crypto Exists
Traditional financial systems can be slow, expensive, and limited by geography. Crypto emerged to enable:
- Borderless value transfer
- 24/7 settlement
- User-controlled custody
- Programmable money via smart contracts

### 1.2 Core Properties
- **Digital scarcity:** Supply is often fixed or algorithmically managed.
- **Verifiability:** Anyone can verify balances/transactions on-chain.
- **Censorship resistance:** Harder for single entities to block transactions.
- **Composability:** Applications can integrate with one another on shared rails.

### 1.3 Categories of Crypto Assets
- **Store-of-value assets** (e.g., BTC-like thesis)
- **Smart contract platforms** (L1s/L2s)
- **Utility tokens** (protocol usage/governance)
- **Stablecoins** (price-pegged assets)
- **Meme/speculative tokens** (high volatility, sentiment-driven)

### 1.4 Investment Perspective
Crypto can offer high upside and high downside. Treat it as a speculative, emerging asset class unless proven otherwise by your own framework.

### 1.5 Practical Exercise
Write a one-page note:
1. Why crypto exists
2. Which category interests you most
3. What risks you are willing to accept

## Chapter 2: Blockchain Fundamentals

A blockchain is a shared database maintained by many participants that agree on valid state transitions.

### 2.1 Blocks, Transactions, and State
- **Transaction:** Instruction to transfer value or execute logic.
- **Block:** Batch of transactions confirmed together.
- **State:** Current ledger snapshot after all valid blocks.

### 2.2 Consensus Mechanisms
- **Proof of Work (PoW):** Security via computation/energy expenditure.
- **Proof of Stake (PoS):** Security via staked capital and validator behavior.

### 2.3 Tradeoffs (The “Blockchain Trilemma”)
Most networks balance:
- Decentralization
- Security
- Scalability

Improving one dimension often impacts others.

### 2.4 Smart Contracts
Programs deployed on-chain that run as written when conditions are met. They enable:
- DeFi
- NFTs
- DAOs
- Automated market logic

### 2.5 Fees and Throughput
- **Gas fees** reflect demand for block space.
- High demand can increase transaction cost.
- Scaling solutions (L2s, rollups, sidechains) attempt to reduce cost and increase speed.

### 2.6 Practical Exercise
Compare 2 chains across:
- Transaction speed
- Average fees
- Validator distribution
- Developer activity

## Chapter 3: Risk Management First

Risk management is your survival system. Returns matter only if you stay in the game.

### 3.1 Position Sizing
Use predetermined sizing rules:
- Max % per single asset
- Max % in speculative bucket
- Max drawdown tolerance

### 3.2 Diversification
Diversify across:
- Asset types (L1, L2, infra, stablecoins)
- Time (DCA)
- Strategy (long-term core + tactical satellite)

### 3.3 Scenario Planning
Pre-define responses for:
- 20% market drop
- 50% drawdown
- Exchange outage
- Stablecoin depeg
- Regulatory headline shock

### 3.4 Risk Controls Checklist
- [ ] Written investment policy
- [ ] Position-size limits
- [ ] Rebalance schedule
- [ ] Security plan
- [ ] Exit criteria

### 3.5 Psychology and Discipline
Common emotional traps:
- FOMO buying
- Revenge trading
- Thesis drift
- Narrative addiction

Use journals and checklists to reduce impulsive decisions.

## Chapter 4: Wallets, Exchanges, and Security

Security is not optional in crypto; custody mistakes can be irreversible.

### 4.1 Custody Models
- **Custodial:** Exchange holds keys (convenient, counterparty risk).
- **Non-custodial:** You hold keys (control, higher responsibility).

### 4.2 Wallet Types
- **Hot wallets:** Connected to internet; convenient, higher attack surface.
- **Cold wallets (hardware):** Offline signing; strongest everyday protection for long-term holdings.

### 4.3 Seed Phrase Management
- Write offline only
- Never store in cloud notes/email
- Keep secure backups in separate physical locations
- Never share with anyone

### 4.4 Exchange Security Best Practices
- Enable strong 2FA (auth app preferred)
- Use withdrawal allowlists
- Separate trading and long-term storage
- Rotate passwords and review sessions/devices

### 4.5 Scam Defense Framework
Watch for:
- “Guaranteed returns”
- Fake support agents
- Urgent “verify wallet” links
- Airdrop drainers
- Impersonation accounts

Rule: if rushed, pause.

## Chapter 5: How to Evaluate Crypto Projects

Use a repeatable framework. Avoid investing on hype alone.

### 5.1 Fundamental Questions
1. What problem is solved?
2. Why blockchain is needed?
3. Who are users?
4. What is the revenue/value accrual path?

### 5.2 Team and Governance
- Public track records
- Execution history
- Governance transparency
- Treasury controls and runway

### 5.3 Tokenomics Review
- Supply schedule and unlocks
- Insider allocation
- Utility vs speculation
- Emissions and dilution pressure

### 5.4 On-Chain and Ecosystem Signals
- Active addresses
- Transaction growth quality
- TVL composition (if DeFi)
- Developer commits/releases
- Partner integrations

### 5.5 Red Flags
- Unclear documentation
- Anonymous team + aggressive marketing
- No credible audits
- Circular yield incentives
- Sudden narrative pivots

### 5.6 Scoring Model (Example)
Score each category 1–5:
- Problem/market
- Team/execution
- Tokenomics
- Adoption
- Risk profile

Invest only above your minimum threshold.

## Chapter 6: Building an Investment Strategy

Your strategy should match your goals, temperament, and time commitment.

### 6.1 Define Objectives
- Capital growth?
- Learning exposure?
- Long-term asymmetric bets?
- Income/yield (with caution)?

### 6.2 Strategy Archetypes
- **DCA Core:** Periodic buys in high-conviction assets.
- **Core-Satellite:** Stable core + smaller tactical bets.
- **Thematic Basket:** Invest by themes (AI x crypto, scaling, infra).

### 6.3 Entry and Exit Rules
Define before buying:
- Entry conditions
- Invalid thesis conditions
- Profit-taking rules
- Maximum holding period without progress

### 6.4 Review Cadence
- Weekly: risk and security checks
- Monthly: allocation review
- Quarterly: thesis validation
- Annual: strategy reset

### 6.5 What to Avoid
- Constant strategy switching
- Overtrading noise
- Increasing risk after losses

## Chapter 7: Portfolio Construction and Rebalancing

Portfolio structure turns ideas into controlled exposure.

### 7.1 Sample Allocation Framework
(Adjust to your risk profile)
- 50–70% Core large-cap exposure
- 15–30% Growth/mid-cap
- 5–15% High-risk/speculative
- 5–20% Stable reserve

### 7.2 Correlation Awareness
Many assets move together in risk-off periods. Apparent diversification may fail during crashes.

### 7.3 Rebalancing Methods
- **Calendar-based:** Monthly/quarterly
- **Threshold-based:** Rebalance when allocation drifts by X%

### 7.4 Liquidity and Execution
Use limit orders where possible.
Consider:
- Slippage
- Spread
- Market depth
- Fee tiers

### 7.5 Portfolio Health Dashboard
Track:
- Total return vs benchmark
- Drawdown
- Volatility
- Win/loss ratio by thesis quality
- Cash/stable reserve ratio

## Chapter 8: Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Mistakes are predictable. Build systems to prevent repeated errors.

### 8.1 Top Errors
- Buying narratives without data
- Ignoring unlock schedules
- Keeping all funds on exchanges
- No documented thesis
- No risk limits

### 8.2 Process Fixes
- Pre-trade checklist
- Post-trade journal
- “Two-source rule” for new claims
- Cooldown timer before large trades

### 8.3 Information Hygiene
Curate input sources:
- Primary docs
- On-chain dashboards
- Developer channels
- Credible long-form research

Reduce noise from hype feeds.

### 8.4 Personal Anti-Error Rules (Example)
- Never allocate >5% to unproven tokens
- No leverage for long-term portfolio
- No buying after >30% daily pump
- Reassess thesis on major governance/security event

## Chapter 9: Taxes, Regulation, and Recordkeeping

Compliance protects you from future legal and financial pain.

### 9.1 Tax Awareness
Tax treatment varies by jurisdiction and can change. Common taxable events may include:
- Selling crypto for fiat
- Swapping one token for another
- Certain staking/reward events
- Spending crypto

### 9.2 Recordkeeping System
Maintain:
- Transaction date/time
- Asset amount
- Cost basis
- Fees paid
- Wallet/exchange source
- Purpose/notes

### 9.3 Regulatory Awareness
Monitor:
- Exchange licensing updates
- Stablecoin rules
- Securities classifications
- Reporting obligations

### 9.4 Professional Support
Work with a qualified crypto-aware tax professional when activity grows complex.

## Glossary

- **Altcoin:** Any crypto asset other than Bitcoin (context-dependent).  
- **ATH:** All-time high price.  
- **Block:** A confirmed batch of transactions.  
- **Consensus:** Mechanism for agreeing on valid chain state.  
- **DCA:** Dollar-cost averaging over time.  
- **Gas Fee:** Cost to execute transactions on-chain.  
- **Private Key:** Secret that controls wallet funds.  
- **Seed Phrase:** Backup words to recover wallet access.  
- **Slippage:** Difference between expected and executed price.  
- **TVL:** Total value locked in protocol smart contracts.  
- **Volatility:** Magnitude/frequency of price fluctuations.
